1. Reflective Activity 1 - Ethics in Computing in the Age of Generative AI

Description: Ethics in Computing in the age of Generative AI

Read Correa et al. (2023) and Deckard (2023).

From late 2022, generative AI has taken the world by storm, and there is no field of activity that has not been impacted in some way. This is so much truer for Computer Science, which is where it all began. It is important to realise, however, that AI itself is nothing new, per se; and if the renaissance of the field after the ‘winter’ of the 1980s has been slow but constant, today there is the need of a different set of rules.

In the Correa et al (2023) paper, the authors state that “a lot of work is taking place to define the values and ideas that should guide AI advances. A key challenge, however, lies in establishing a consensus on these values, given the diverse perspectives of various stakeholders worldwide and the abstraction of normative discourse. Researchers and policy makers need better tools to catalogue and compare AI governance documents from around the world and to identify points of divergence and commonality.”

After reviewing the article and reading how different countries across the world deal with the generative AI revolution, discuss your views on the subject and recommend what you think could be a suitable course of action. You should justify your stance by also reviewing any papers included in this study or other relevant literature (additional links to industry have been provided as ‘Other Resources’ to the module). Your discussion should also highlight the impact your actions would have on applicable legal, social and professional issues. Please note that there is no right or wrong answer here, this exercise is to help you evaluate the legal, social ethical and professional issues that affect computing professionals in industry.

3. Summary of Collaborative Discussion 1

The Accessibility in Software Development case study highlights the ethical dilemma of balancing client deadlines with the responsibility to create inclusive products. My initial analysis emphasised that omitting accessibility features risks discriminating against users with disabilities, violating ACM Principles 1.1 (contribute to society) and 1.4 (act fairly and avoid discrimination) (ACM, 2018), as well as BCS duties to uphold equality, diversity, and public interest (BCS, 2024). Such omissions can also trigger legal consequences under frameworks like the UK Equality Act 2010 and WCAG standards (W3C, 2018).

Peer feedback from Julius Cloos deepened this perspective by highlighting ACM Principle 1.2, which stresses adherence to best practices, and Principle 2.3, which encourages following professional standards to avoid wasted effort on inaccessible features (Horton, 2025). Implementing accessibility from the outset not only aligns with ethical codes, but also prevents inefficiencies and reputational damage.

The peer feedback from Md Chowdhury reinforced that accessibility should be treated as a core quality attribute, comparable to security or performance. Building it in early reduces technical debt, minimises costly rework, and demonstrates a commitment to inclusive design as a business value. This framing can help secure stakeholder buy-in, even under tight deadlines.

Both the ACM and BCS codes frame accessibility as an ethical imperative, not an optional enhancement. The discussion confirms that proactive, integrated accessibility practices support legal compliance, uphold professional integrity, and strengthen trust with users. Balancing commercial pressures with these responsibilities ensures technology serves all members of society, fulfilling the broader purpose of the computing profession.

4. Summary of Collaborative Discussion 2

The case of Abi and the Whizzz cereal study has prompted a valuable exchange on integrity, responsibility, and the pressures of commercial interest. My initial post argued that Abi’s duty is to present the data transparently, reflecting both positive and negative findings. This position aligns with the ACM Code of Ethics (2018) and the BCS Code of Conduct (2024), which stress honesty, the avoidance of harm, and the protection of the public interest.

Valentina’s responses reinforced that data integrity is fundamental to sustaining public trust in science. Once findings are perceived as biased or manipulated, credibility is difficult to restore. This aligns with O’Neil’s (2016) critique that misapplied statistical models can have far-reaching societal consequences. The point that responsibility extends beyond one company or study is well made; the perception of bias can erode confidence in entire professions.

Valentina also asked whether Abi could ever justify prioritising the manufacturer’s interests, for instance if the “harmful” results came from a small sample or weak statistical test. I would argue that even in such cases, Abi cannot ethically suppress or downplay those results. Instead, he should report them transparently with clear caveats about sample size, robustness, and uncertainty. The European Food Safety Authority (2019) guidance on uncertainty emphasises that limitations must be communicated explicitly so stakeholders understand the strength of the evidence. Suppressing unfavourable outcomes risks misleading stakeholders, violating ethical codes, and undermining trust.

In conclusion, Abi’s responsibility is not only technical but moral. By reporting fully and contextualising uncertainty, he upholds professional integrity, safeguards public health, and protects the credibility of both science and computing practice.

12. Reflections on Working with the Research and Professional Practice Module

Introduction

This reflection summarises my learning journey through the Research Methods and Professional Practice module. I will use the "What?, So what?, Now what?" model by Rolfe et al. (2001), as outlined in the University of Edinburgh’s Reflectors’ Toolkit (University of Edinburgh, n.d.). The module has been one of the most valuable parts of my studies so far because it helped me connect theory, analysis, and professional practice in a meaningful way. It challenged me to think more critically, apply research metho ds with confidence, and reflect on my growth as both a student and working as a professional software quality test engineer.

WHAT?

At the start of this module, my understanding of research was fairly limited. I had experience with processes performed when conducting research, such as collecting data and analysing it. However, as the module progressed, I began to see research as a structured and reflective process that requires planning, justification, and ethical consideration.

Developing my literature review and research proposal was one of the most important milestones. My topic focused on building a sentiment-analysis framework using machine learning to process customer feedback. Initially, my work was descriptive, summarising what others had done. After receiving tutor feedback, I realised that I needed to go beyond description and engage critically with methods, datasets, and limitations. This helped me focus the proposal on key research gaps such as interpretability and domain adaptation, and on practical approaches like using BERT and explainable-AI tools such as LIME and SHAP.

Activities and exercises involving analytics, such as those on summary measures and hypothesis testing, helped me gain hands-on experience with descriptive and inferential statistics. Using Excel functions like AVERAGE, STDEV, and T.TEST, I learned how to calculate and interpret measures such as means, standard deviations, and p-values using Excel. These tasks made me realise how statistical evidence supports decision-making and how much precision matters when interpreting data.

The data-analysis exercises were also very insightful. Working through the chi-square and t-test worksheets, I saw how theory translates into actual analysis. These examples gave me confidence in selecting appropriate tests, checking assumptions, and interpreting statistical results correctly.

Alongside these tasks, I used the skills matrix and action plan to reflect on my development. I identified clear improvements in areas such as data handling, use of academic sources, and applying Harvard referencing consistently. By the end of the module, I had moved from seeing research as a mechanical task to understanding it as an intellectual process that combines logic, creativity, and reflection.

SO WHAT?

Looking back, this module has changed how I think about research and evidence. I have come to understand that research is about reasoning and justification, not just calculation. Through the exercises, I developed a much deeper appreciation for how data supports conclusions. For example, when interpreting the results of the t-tests, I realised that small numerical differences could still be statistically meaningful depending on variability and sample size. This has made me more careful when drawing conclusions, both in my studies and at work.

The literature review process was especially transformative. I learned to read more critically and to look for the reasoning behind methodological choices. Instead of taking research findings at face value, I began to ask why certain models were used, how datasets were selected, and what implications the results had. This shift in thinking helped me improve my own writing by focusing more on argument and evaluation rather than summary. It also made me aware of common issues such as bias in datasets, overfitting, and the challenges of interpretability in AI models. As Cottrell (2019) notes, critical reflection means moving beyond description to evaluation and judgement, which I now see as an essential academic and professional skill.

I also learned a lot from feedback, I revisited them with a reflective mindset to understand their value. Feedback encouraged me to refine my scope, improve structure, and justify methodological decisions. Following Dweck’s (2017) concept of a growth mindset, I learned to view feedback as part of the learning process rather than as a judgment.

Another area of growth has been ethics and professional practice. Engaging with the ACM (2018) and BCS (2024) codes of conduct reminded me that ethical responsibility is central to both research and software engineering. Whether handling user data or designing automated systems, it is crucial to ensure fairness, privacy, and transparency. This has changed how I approach testing and data handling at work. I now question how algorithms might produce bias and how data is stored or shared.

Overall, this module helped me transition from being a passive learner to an active, reflective practitioner. It taught me that good research, like good testing, depends on curiosity, rigour, and continuous evaluation.

NOW WHAT?

As I move into my next module, the Master’s dissertation, I feel much better prepared to design and conduct independent research. I now have the confidence to use appropriate methods, justify them clearly, and apply them rigorously when threading into the next and final module. I will be prepared to conduct processes, such as collecting and analysing real data and use statistical skills I developed here, such as conducting t-tests, correlation analysis, and significance testing.

I also intend to maintain the reflective habits developed through this module will help me document progress, challenges, and decisions throughout my dissertation. Reflection will keep me aware of the reasoning behind each step and help me identify improvements along the way.

In my professional career, the lessons from this module have already started influencing how I work. In many ways, software testing can be approached as a form of research, framing hypotheses, collecting data, testing outcomes, and interpreting results objectively. I am also more aware of ethical and social factors in technology, especially around bias, data protection, and explainability. These insights have made my professional practice more thoughtful and responsible.

For my ongoing development, I aim to expand my technical skills by using Python’s data-analysis libraries (such as pandas and SciPy) to handle larger datasets more efficiently. I also plan to keep improving my knowledge of research ethics, especially around AI transparency and data privacy.

Most importantly, this module has given me a strong foundation for becoming a reflective computing professional. I now see reflection not as an afterthought but as an essential part of learning and practice. As I progress towards my dissertation and beyond, I will continue to use reflective thinking to connect theory, data, and ethical awareness. This mindset will not only help me succeed academically but also make me a more capable and conscientious engineer.
