1. Summary of Collaborative Discussion 1 - Report on Ryanair and Easyjet Websites

This report compared the route map visualizations of Ryanair and EasyJet websites, focusing on their effectiveness in aiding travel planning. Initially, the analysis examined attributes like country names, locations, and connections, abstracting the task as understanding and planning travel routes with a focus on departure and destination airports. Ryanair’s visualization also included price levels.

The data representation in both cases utilized points, lines, and areas to depict locations, routes, and countries, respectively. However, Ryanair employed point thickness to indicate route importance, adding a layer of information. EasyJet’s clear color scheme, distinguishing departure (orange) and destination (black), was noted as simple and intuitive. Ryanair, while also using color effectively, relied more on brand colors and point thickness, effectively differentiating “task-data” from “informational data”, and incorporating price and a “Book flight” button, simplifying the booking process.

Feedback from Nawaz Khan prompted a deeper dive into Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) principles. Both websites, while effective, could improve their adherence to HCI best practices. For example, the potential visual clutter in dense route areas, a limitation noted in both visualizations, directly impacts usability, violating the principle of minimizing cognitive load (Whitenton, 2018)

Matthew Bowyer’s request for more in-depth clarity highlighted the need to expand on the user perspective. Ryanair’s inclusion of price and booking options aligns with the HCI goal of efficiency, streamlining the user’s task. EasyJet's simpler design, though intuitive, could benefit from more interactive elements, enhancing engagement and usability.

To address the clutter issue, both airlines could implement filtering options, allowing users to focus on specific routes or destinations. Furthermore, EasyJet could enhance its design by providing more immediate information upon hover or click, reducing the need for multiple interactions. Ryanair, while successful in providing price information, could improve its visual hierarchy to prevent information overload. Ultimately, both websites need to balance visual appeal with functional clarity to enhance user experience.

References:

  • Whitenton, K. (2013) Minimize Cognitive Load to Maximize Usability. Available at: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/minimize-cognitive-load/ (Accessed: February 24th, 2025).
  • Munzner, T. (2014). Visualization analysis and design. CRC press.
  • Norman, D. A. (2013). The design of everyday things: Revised and expanded edition. Basic Books.
  • Few, S. (2006). Information dashboard design: The essential guide to effective communication of data. O'Reilly Media, Inc.
